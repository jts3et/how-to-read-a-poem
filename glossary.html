Glossary

The working vocabulary of meter, rhyme, and form.

Meter & scansion

ProsodyThe study of versification — meter, rhythm, and the patterning of sound.
ScansionMarking a line’s stressed and unstressed syllables to reveal its meter.
MeterThe regular pattern of stressed and slack syllables a line is measured against.
RhythmThe actual movement of stress in speech, heard against (and often bending) the meter.
Quantitative meterMeter by syllable duration, long and short — the Greek and Latin system.
Accentual meterMeter by number of stresses, ignoring syllable count — the Old English system.
Accentual-syllabicMeter counting both stress and syllable — the English norm since Chaucer.
IctusThe metrical beat: the stressed position within a foot.
PromotionA normally weak syllable given stress because the meter calls for it.
DemotionA normally strong syllable left unstressed by the meter.
SubstitutionReplacing the expected foot with a different one for effect.
ElisionSliding two syllables into one (heav’n, temp’rate) to keep the count.

The foot & the line

FootThe basic metrical unit — one stressed syllable with its slacks.
Iambu S — to-DAY. The commonest English foot.
TrocheeS u — GAR-den. A falling foot.
Anapestu u S — in-ter-VENE.
DactylS u u — MER-ri-ly. The foot of classical epic.
SpondeeS S — HEART-BREAK. Two stresses.
Pyrrhicu u — two slacks.
Line lengthsmonometer (1), dimeter (2), trimeter (3), tetrameter (4), pentameter (5), hexameter (6), heptameter (7).
AlexandrineA six-foot iambic line (iambic hexameter).
CaesuraA pause within a line.
EnjambmentA sentence running past the line-end without pause.
End-stoppedA line whose end falls on a grammatical pause.
CatalexisA line missing its final slack syllable (a catalectic line).
Acephalous“Headless” — a line missing its first slack syllable.
Feminine endingAn extra slack syllable after the final stress.
Masculine endingA line ending on a stress.

Rhyme & sound

Rhyme schemeThe pattern of end-rhymes, lettered (ABAB, ABBA…).
Perfect rhymeMatching stressed vowel and everything after (day / May).
Slant (half) rhymeApproximate rhyme (soul / all).
Eye rhymeRhyme by spelling, not sound (love / move).
Masculine rhymeRhyme on a final stressed syllable.
Feminine rhymeRhyme on a stressed + slack syllable (motion / ocean).
Internal rhymeRhyme within a line rather than at its end.
AlliterationRepeated initial consonant sounds — the binding of Old English verse.
AssonanceRepeated vowel sounds.
ConsonanceRepeated consonant sounds.
RefrainA line or phrase repeated at intervals.

Stanzas & forms

StanzaA group of lines set off as a unit.
Couplet / tercet / quatrainStanzas of two, three, and four lines.
Octave / sestetThe eight-line and six-line parts of a sonnet.
VoltaThe turn of thought in a sonnet, usually at line 9.
SonnetFourteen pentameter lines with a volta — Shakespearean (English), Spenserian, or Petrarchan (Italian).
Blank verseUnrhymed iambic pentameter.
Heroic coupletA closed, rhyming iambic-pentameter couplet.
Rhyme royalA seven-line pentameter stanza, ababbcc — Chaucer’s.
Spenserian stanzaEight pentameters and a closing alexandrine, ABABBCBCC.
Terza rimaInterlocking tercets, ABA BCB CDC…
Common measureAlternating four- and three-beat lines — the ballad and hymn stanza.
OdeA long lyric of elevated address and feeling.
VillanelleNineteen lines on two refrains — five tercets and a quatrain.
Free verse (vers libre)Verse without regular meter or rhyme.

Genres & modes

LyricA short poem of a single voice and feeling.
DramaticVerse written to be spoken by a character.
EpicA long narrative poem of heroic action.
Dramatic monologueA poem spoken by a character to an implied, silent listener.
Auditory imaginationEliot’s term for the feeling for syllable and rhythm below conscious thought.
