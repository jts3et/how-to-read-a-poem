Sweeney Agonistes

T. S. Eliot, Fragments of an Aristophanic Melodrama (1926–27). We open here.

The exercise. Before anyone hears Eliot read it, volunteers take the parts and read the Fragment of an Agon aloud, cold. Read for the sense, or read for the beat, and notice which one you reach for. The jazz rhythm, the refrains, the split lines across speakers, none of it comes with instructions. What you do with it is already a set of decisions about how a poem wants to be voiced.

Presenter: cast the parts (Sweeney, Wauchope, Horsfall, Klipstein, Krumpacker, Doris, Dusty). Read the second fragment. Do not coach delivery — the uncoached variety is the material for step 3.

The text

T. S. Eliot, Sweeney Agonistes. Shown for classroom reading. Open the PDF in a new tab ↗

Then: Eliot reads

After the room has made its own choices, we play Eliot's own recording and hear how strange his delivery is, the liturgical drone that turns a jazz melodrama into something else. The point is not that his reading is correct. It is that his reading, too, is a decision.
