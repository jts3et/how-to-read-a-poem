The Waste Land

Hour 2. T. S. Eliot, 1922. Read aloud, whole room, each delivery a claim to defend.

Where the first hour installed a pentameter to hear, the second hour listens for it ghosting under free verse. We work a handful of passages together and argue the deliveries out, treating where a voice turns, or pauses, or repeats, as a reading of the poem and not a matter of taste.

The text

T. S. Eliot, The Waste Land (1922). Public domain. Open the PDF in a new tab ↗

Passages we work

  • The opening seven lines — the participial line-endings (breeding, mixing, stirring) and what they do to forward motion; whether anyone can still say "April is the cruellest month" straight.
  • The hyacinth garden — where the voice turns.
  • Unreal City — the crowd flowing over London Bridge; a pentameter with a feminine ending, blank verse under free verse.
  • "HURRY UP PLEASE ITS TIME" — whether it takes the same voice each time it recurs.
  • Death by Water (Phlebas) — short, self-contained, a test of pace.
  • What the Thunder Said — DA / Datta / Dayadhvam / Damyata.

Eliot reads

Recording embed to be set (Eliot's 1946 reading). Best played after the room has already argued a passage, so the drone lands as one more contestable delivery.

Discussion threads (adjudication resources)

  • Voices — the drafts' original title, He Do the Police in Different Voices; the Tiresias note ("what Tiresias sees, in fact, is the substance of the poem").
  • Method — Pound's cuts; quotation as the compositional unit.
  • Theory — impersonality and the catalyst (Tradition and the Individual Talent, 1919); the auditory imagination, Eliot's own phrase from The Use of Poetry and the Use of Criticism.

Let each enter only when a dispute calls for it: voices when the recurring lines force the question, Pound when someone asks why a transition is missing, impersonality when someone asks whose feelings the poem expresses.

Hour-2 shape is provisional — swap the passage set or the threads as you like.
