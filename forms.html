Verse forms

Meter measures the line; form organizes lines into a whole — a rhyme scheme, a stanza, a shape the tradition recognizes.

A form is a contract about arrangement. The sonnet promises fourteen lines and a turn of thought; the couplet promises a closing click of rhyme; blank verse promises the pentameter without the rhyme. Naming the form tells you what the poem has agreed to do before it does it, and where to listen for the moment it strains against its own agreement.

The sonnet, three ways

Fourteen lines of iambic pentameter, an argument with a turn (the volta). Three rhyme schemes competed as the form entered English from Italy in the sixteenth century, and the scheme shapes where the turn can fall.

Shakespearean (English) — three quatrains and a couplet

Rhyme ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. Three four-line units build the case; the closing couplet clinches or twists it. The turn usually waits for line 9, and the couplet gets the last word.

Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day? Thou art more lovely and more temperate: Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May, And summer’s lease hath all too short a date; Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines, And often is his gold complexion dimm’d; And every fair from fair sometime declines, By chance or nature’s changing course untrimm’d; But thy eternal summer shall not fade, Nor lose possession of that fair thou ow’st; Nor shall Death brag thou wander’st in his shade, When in eternal lines to time thou grow’st: So long as men can breathe or eyes can see, So long lives this, and this gives life to thee. — Shakespeare, Sonnet 18 (1609)

Spenserian — the quatrains interlocked

Rhyme ABAB BCBC CDCD EE. Spenser chains the quatrains, carrying each rhyme forward into the next, so the poem pulls through itself rather than resetting every four lines.

One day I wrote her name upon the strand, But came the waves and washèd it away: Again I wrote it with a second hand, But came the tide, and made my pains his prey. “Vain man,” said she, “that dost in vain assay A mortal thing so to immortalize; For I myself shall like to this decay, And eek my name be wipèd out likewise.” “Not so,” quod I, “let baser things devise To die in dust, but you shall live by fame: My verse your virtues rare shall eternize, And in the heavens write your glorious name: Where whenas Death shall all the world subdue, Our love shall live, and later life renew. — Spenser, Amoretti 75 (1595)

Petrarchan (Italian) — octave and sestet

Rhyme ABBAABBA, then a sestet (here CDDECE). The eight-line octave sets a problem; the turn falls at line 9; the six-line sestet answers. Christina Rossetti hinges the whole grief of the poem on that turn.

Remember me when I am gone away, Gone far away into the silent land; When you can no more hold me by the hand, Nor I half turn to go yet turning stay. Remember me when no more day by day You tell me of our future that you plann’d: Only remember me; you understand It will be late to counsel then or pray. Yet if you should forget me for a while And afterwards remember, do not grieve: For if the darkness and corruption leave A vestige of the thoughts that once I had, Better by far you should forget and smile Than that you should remember and be sad. — Christina Rossetti, “Remember” (1862)

Forms across the centuries

The counted line, once settled, organizes itself into a repertory that runs from the Renaissance through the nineteenth century.

Blank verse — unrhymed iambic pentameter (16th c. on)

Surrey’s invention becomes the line of English drama and epic, speech-like because unrhymed.

Of Man’s first disobedience, and the fruit Of that forbidden tree whose mortal taste Brought death into the world, and all our woe — Milton, Paradise Lost (1667)

Heroic couplet — closed rhyming pentameter (17th–18th c.)

The couplet snaps shut on a balanced antithesis, the Augustan workhorse for wit and argument.

True wit is Nature to advantage dress’d, What oft was thought, but ne’er so well express’d. — Pope, An Essay on Criticism (1711)

Common measure — the ballad and hymn stanza

Alternating four-beat and three-beat lines, rhyming on the short lines: the meter of ballads and hymns, and — bent to her own ends — of Emily Dickinson.

Because I could not stop for Death – He kindly stopped for me – The Carriage held but just Ourselves – And Immortality. — Emily Dickinson (c.1863)

The Spenserian stanza (16th c.; revived by the Romantics)

Eight pentameter lines and a closing alexandrine (a six-foot line), rhyming ABABBCBCC, the long stanza Keats reaches for to slow narrative down.

St. Agnes’ Eve — Ah, bitter chill it was! The owl, for all his feathers, was a-cold; The hare limp’d trembling through the frozen grass — Keats, The Eve of St. Agnes (1820)

Terza rima — interlocking tercets (Romantic)

Three-line stanzas chained ABA BCB CDC, borrowed from Dante, driving relentlessly forward.

O wild West Wind, thou breath of Autumn’s being, Thou, from whose unseen presence the leaves dead Are driven, like ghosts from an enchanter fleeing — Shelley, Ode to the West Wind (1820)
All texts are public domain. The three sonnet forms trace the shape from Wyatt and Surrey in the 1530s through Spenser and Shakespeare to Christina Rossetti; the wider repertory runs from Milton to the Romantics and Victorians.
