What just happened when you read that aloud

Reading a poem out loud runs on skills you mostly cannot state.

Some of what you drew on is explicit and teachable. You know, or could look up, how the words are pronounced, what a refrain is, which allusions are in play, where the line breaks fall and what a broken line across two speakers is asking for. Prosody, versification, literary history, convention: this is the part of reading that lives in books and can be handed over in a sentence.

The rest is tacit, and it lives in the body. Where you breathed, how you leaned on one word and threw away the next, what your throat and jaw did to shape a vowel, how fast you let a line run before the sense made you slow, none of that arrived as a rule. You performed it before you could describe it, the way a cyclist balances without being able to write down the corrections. Michael Polanyi called this the knowing that exceeds what we can say.

The two pull against each other in the mouth. Read for meaning and you follow the grammar, pausing where the thought pauses. Read for stress and you follow the beat, letting the meter promote a small word into prominence the sense would have skipped over. A good reading holds both at once and lets them argue. The whole of the next hour is about making that argument audible, and about giving you an explicit hold on the part of it that is meter.

A reason this matters now

If reading well is a real skill, so is writing well, and we tend to assume we can tell the difference. A 2024 study suggests the assumption is shaky.

The study Porter & Machery (2024). "AI-Generated Poetry Is Indistinguishable from Human-Written Poetry and Is Rated More Favorably," Scientific Reports 14. University of Pittsburgh, History & Philosophy of Science. doi:10.1038/s41598-024-76900-1.

Method

They took ten famous English-language poets across the tradition, from Chaucer and Shakespeare through Dickinson, Whitman, Byron, and Eliot, and had a current chatbot generate poems "in the style of" each. Then they showed the poems to 1,634 non-expert readers. In the first task readers simply judged, poem by poem, whether each was written by a human or by AI. In the second, a fresh group of readers rated the poems on qualities like rhythm, beauty, emotion, and originality, without being told anything about where the poems came from.

Results

  • Below chance at telling them apart. Readers identified the AI poems correctly only 46.6% of the time, worse than a coin toss. They were more likely to call an AI poem "human" than to call an actual human poem "human."
  • The AI poems were preferred. When nobody knew the source, the AI poems scored higher on rhythm, beauty, and the other qualities, and that higher rating was part of why readers mistook them for human, they reasoned that a poem this competent and accessible must be by a person, while the real Dickinson or Eliot read as strange and got filed under "machine."

What it does and does not show

It does not show that the machine writes better poems. It shows that non-expert readers, judging fast and alone, reach for surface fluency as their tell, and that surface fluency is exactly what the model supplies. The skill that would catch the difference, hearing where a real poem's rhythm resists you, where its strangeness is doing work, is the tacit, embodied part we started with, and it is unevenly distributed and rarely taught. That is the gap this workshop pokes at. Learning to scan will not settle whether a poem is any good, but it gives the ear something explicit to hold, so that the strangeness of a real line becomes information rather than noise.
