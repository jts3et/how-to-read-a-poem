Where the line comes from

Three systems of meter, each with a text to scan and, where it survives, a voice to hear.

Quantitative — length, not stress

Greek and Latin measure syllables by duration, long (—) or short (∪). The epic line is the dactylic hexameter: six feet, each a dactyl (— ∪ ∪) or spondee (— —), the last foot — X.

Ar-ma vi- | rum-que ca- | nō Trō- | iae quī | prī-mus ab | ō-rīs dactyl dactyl spondee spondee dactyl spondee — Virgil, Aeneid 1.1
Arma virumque canō, Trōiae quī prīmus ab ōrīs, Ītaliam fātō profugus Lāvīniaque vēnit lītora, multum ille et terrīs iactātus et altō vī superum, saevae memorem Iūnōnis ob īram. — Virgil, Aeneid 1.1–4
μῆνιν ἄειδε θεά, Πηληϊάδεω Ἀχιλῆος οὐλομένην, ἣ μυρί’ Ἀχαιοῖς ἄλγε’ ἔθηκε (mēnin aeide theā Pēlēïadeō Achilēos…) — Homer, Iliad 1.1–2

The Iliad in ancient Greek   Hear the running dactyls — the meter is duration, not stress.

Accentual — count the stresses

Old English counts four strong beats a line, bound by alliteration across a central pause, with any number of slacks between.

Hwæt! Wē Gār-Dena in gēar-dagum, þēod-cyninga þrym gefrūnon, hū ðā æþelingas ellen fremedon. — Beowulf 1–3

Benjamin Bagby, Beowulf with the lyre

Accentual-syllabic — the settlement

Chaucer brings a counted line into English; the final -e still sounds. By Shakespeare, stress and syllable are both counted, and variation is heard against a steady norm.

Whan that Aprille with his shoures soote The droghte of March hath perced to the roote — Chaucer, General Prologue 1–2
