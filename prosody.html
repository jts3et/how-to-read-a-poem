Where the English line comes from

Three systems of meter, and the history that carried English from one to the next.

English verse did not always sound the way a Shakespeare sonnet sounds. The regular alternation of light and heavy syllables we now hear as "poetry" is a settlement reached in the sixteenth century, on top of an older native rhythm and under pressure from imported classical models. Hearing the earlier stages is the fastest way to understand what the settled line is doing.

Three systems

Quantitative — length, not stress

Greek and Latin meter counts syllables as long or short by duration, in fixed patterns. Virgil's line is dactylic hexameter, six feet of long-short-short.

Arma virumque canō, Trōiae quī prīmus ab ōrīs — Virgil, Aeneid 1.1

English never took to this — our language organizes rhythm by stress, not length — but Renaissance poets kept trying, and the failure is instructive.

Accentual — count the stresses

Old English verse counts stresses, not syllables: four strong beats a line, bound by alliteration across a central pause, with any number of slack syllables between. This is Beowulf.

Hwæt! Wē Gār-Dena in gēar-dagum, þēod-cyninga þrym gefrūnon — Beowulf, ll. 1–2

Accentual-syllabic — the settlement

Chaucer, hearing French and Italian syllable-counting, brings a counted line into English. The final -e is still sounded, so the pentameter is audible if you let the endings ring.

Whan that Aprille with his shoures soote The droghte of March hath perced to the roote — Chaucer, General Prologue, ll. 1–2

By Shakespeare the system is fixed: five iambic feet a line, stress and syllable both counted, variation heard against a steady norm.

Shall I compare thee to a summer's day? Thou art more lovely and more temperate — Shakespeare, Sonnet 18

Forms, modes, and the loosening line

During the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries the counted line meets a flood of imported forms — the sonnet from Italy, the ode, terza and ottava rima — and English poets sort verse into modes: the dramatic (written for a speaking character), the lyric (the singular voice), the epic (the long narrative). Three developments then carry the pentameter toward the modern line.

Blank verse — the unrhymed pentameter

Unrhymed iambic pentameter becomes the engine of English drama and epic, flexible enough to sound like speech.

Was this the face that launch'd a thousand ships, And burnt the topless towers of Ilium? — Marlowe, Doctor Faustus

The dramatic monologue — a character overheard

The nineteenth century turns blank verse into a single speaker caught mid-utterance, the reader positioned as eavesdropper.

That's my last Duchess painted on the wall, Looking as if she were alive. — Browning, My Last Duchess

Vers libre — the norm withdrawn

By the early twentieth century the pentameter is no longer kept, only remembered — free verse plays against a regularity it declines to supply, which is exactly the game the second hour hears in The Waste Land.

Let us go then, you and I, When the evening is spread out against the sky — Eliot, Prufrock (1915)
