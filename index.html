How to Read a Poem

A two-hour workshop. Diverse Intelligences Summer Institute.

Every text, recording, and exercise for the session lives here. Read along on your own screen, or follow the one at the front. The workshop moves from a cold reading of Eliot through the history and mechanics of English verse, and lands on a method you use yourself, scanning real lines on this site while the room compares what it hears.

The two hours

Hour 1 How to read a poem
  • Open with Sweeney Agonistes. Volunteers read the Fragment of an Agon cold, before hearing Eliot, then we play the recording.
  • The point of the exercise. Reading poetry draws on explicit and tacit skill at once, and a recent study finds readers now prefer AI poems to the canon without knowing which is which.
  • Prosody and scansion. Where English meter comes from, heard in Old English, Middle English, and the Renaissance line.
  • Scan for yourself. The method, then live practice on the page.
Hour 2 The Waste Land, read aloud

Whole-room work on the passages, with each delivery treated as a claim to be defended. The voices, the cuts, and impersonality enter as they are needed.

Run of show

Hour 1

  1. Hand out / project Sweeney. Ask for volunteers to read the Fragment of an Agon. Do not coach the delivery.
  2. Play the Eliot recording. Let the difference sit before naming it.
  3. Name the point: explicit + tacit + embodied skill; the meaning-vs-stress tension. Walk the AI-poetry study (46.6% — below chance; AI rated higher on rhythm and beauty).
  4. Prosody: Beowulf (accentual) → Chaucer (the line arriving) → Shakespeare (accentual-syllabic settled). Quantitative / accentual / accentual-syllabic. Verse forms, modes, blank verse → dramatic monologue → vers libre.
  5. Scan: teach 4B4V, then the practice tool. Early lines are Submit — collect and project variation on the Board. Later lines are Check.

Hour 2 — adjudication resources

  • Voices: the drafts' title He Do the Police in Different Voices; the Tiresias note.
  • Method: Pound's cuts; quotation as compositional unit.
  • Theory: impersonality and the catalyst (1919); the auditory imagination (Eliot's own phrase).
