How to scan a poem

A map of the methods, then the one we use, then live practice.

Five ways people scan English verse

MethodWhat it doesGood for / limit
Graphic-foot (Fussell)Cut the line into feet from a fixed set; name the meter by the prevailing foot and length.Naming the contract and hearing variation against it / lays a Greco-Roman grid over a stress language.
Promotion / demotion (Steele)Same feet, but foregrounds relative stress — meter can promote a weak syllable or demote a strong one.The disputes that teach / not a rival method so much as the ear the first one needs.
Beat-and-offbeat (Attridge)Drops classical feet; marks beats and offbeats, four or five beats a line.English as a stress-timed language, loose meters / different vocabulary, coarser on substitutions.
Generative (Halle–Keyser)Meter as an abstract template plus rules deciding whether a line is metrical.Rigour, predicting what a meter permits / technical, built for analysis not performance.
Temporal / musical (Lanier)Scans by time — bars and rests, as in music.Tempo / over-regularizes; largely superseded.

Behind all five sit three systems: quantitative (Greek and Latin, long and short syllables), accentual (Old English, count the stresses), and accentual-syllabic (the Renaissance settlement English still runs on). We use the first method, sharpened by the second.

The method (4B4V)

Six permitted feet

Rising feet (iamb, anapest) are the more common in English; prefer them when the grouping is ambiguous.

The procedure

  1. Mark the polysyllables by dictionary stress (de-SIRE, CON-sum-ing).
  2. Mark the stressed monosyllables — usually nouns, main verbs, adjectives, adverbs, rhyme words.
  3. Leave the rest slack — articles, prepositions, auxiliaries, conjunctions.
  4. Read it back and adjust. Say the line aloud; negotiate between the rule and the speech. Stress is relative — a syllable is stressed against its neighbours.
  5. Group into feet from the six above. Allow the headless line (missing first slack), the catalectic line (missing last slack), and the feminine ending (an extra slack after the last stress).
  6. Name the meter — the prevailing foot across the poem is the metronome; each line's variations play against it.

Be meek to the meter. When the meter wants a stress that prose habit would skip, the promotion usually reveals meaning. Notation, one line:
