Scansion

Six feet, six steps, one rule: be meek to the meter.

  1. Mark the polysyllables by dictionary stress.
  2. Mark the stressed monosyllables (nouns, main verbs, adjectives, adverbs, rhyme words).
  3. Leave the rest slack (articles, prepositions, auxiliaries, conjunctions).
  4. Read it back and adjust — stress is relative, heard against neighbours.
  5. Group into feet; allow headless, catalectic, and feminine endings.
  6. Name the meter — the prevailing foot is the metronome.

A whole sonnet, scanned

An accent ´ marks each stressed syllable (and the stress falls into accent colour); the feet divide by rule, and a word split across a boundary is hyphenated. The plain poem sits beside it, to read.

Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day? Thou art more lovely and more temperate: Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May, And summer’s lease hath all too short a date; Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines, And often is his gold complexion dimm’d; And every fair from fair sometime declines, By chance or nature’s changing course untrimm’d; But thy eternal summer shall not fade, Nor lose possession of that fair thou ow’st; Nor shall Death brag thou wander’st in his shade, When in eternal lines to time thou grow’st: So long as men can breathe or eyes can see, So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.

Iambic pentameter throughout, with a spondee opening line 3 (rough winds) and a trochaic inversion opening line 5 (some-time); every and temperate elide.
